Structured for Institutional Scale

Lunanele Holdings was constituted on a premise that most private enterprises avoid: that the most consequential capital in any economy is patient capital — deployed across long durations, anchored in productive assets, and aligned to national development priorities rather than quarterly reporting cycles.

The group's holding architecture is not an administrative convenience. It is a deliberate governance instrument — designed to centralise strategic authority, consolidate financial oversight, and enable each subsidiary to execute its mandate with institutional precision.

This is a group structured for engagement with entities that require counterparts of substance: development finance institutions, sovereign capital bodies, multilateral organisations, and institutional investors whose mandates demand rigour, transparency, and long-term alignment.

I
Centralised Capital Governance

All capital allocation decisions are made at holding company level. No subsidiary operates outside the group's unified financial framework.

II
Asset-Backed Value Creation

Growth is anchored in tangible, productive assets — land, infrastructure, systems, and licensed operational capacity. No speculative positions. No paper instruments without physical backing.

III
Developmental Alignment

The group's sector portfolio is deliberately aligned to South Africa's developmental priorities — food security, energy infrastructure, digital transformation, and structured financial markets.

IV
Long-Horizon Mandate

Investment decisions are evaluated against decade-scale value creation. The group does not optimise for short-term yield. It builds for generational durability.

South Africa's Infrastructure Deficit
Infrastructure · Position Paper · Lunanele Holdings

Executive Summary

South Africa's infrastructure backlog is not a new observation — it is a structural reality that has deepened over two decades of underspending, procurement failures, and institutional capacity erosion. The National Infrastructure Plan identifies a requirement exceeding R4.3 trillion in infrastructure investment over the next fifteen years. Public budgets, constrained by fiscal consolidation and debt servicing obligations, cannot absorb this demand.

The Private Capital Imperative

The shortfall between what state budgets can deploy and what the infrastructure programme requires is not a temporary gap — it is a permanent structural condition. Private capital, particularly capital held within disciplined holding structures with long-horizon mandates, is the only vehicle capable of filling this gap at the required scale and speed.

The holding company model — as opposed to the project-specific SPV or the single-sector fund — offers institutional investors a structurally superior vehicle for infrastructure exposure. It provides diversification across infrastructure sub-sectors, centralised governance, and the operational continuity that individual project vehicles cannot sustain across decade-scale development timelines.

Lunanele Infrastructure's Positioning

Lunanele Infrastructure Pty Ltd is constituted specifically to serve as such a vehicle. Its mandate encompasses civil construction, energy systems, and industrial facilities — three of the highest-priority sub-sectors in the National Infrastructure Plan. Its ownership by Lunanele Holdings ensures that capital deployed through the subsidiary is governed at group level, with consolidated financial oversight and strategic continuity that project-level vehicles cannot provide.

Conclusion

The infrastructure deficit is a problem of scale that only private capital, structured appropriately and deployed with institutional discipline, can address. The holding company model is not simply one option among many — it is the structurally correct vehicle for the challenge South Africa faces. Lunanele Infrastructure represents this vehicle in its earliest and most accessible form.

Cold Chain as Strategic National Infrastructure
Agribusiness · Position Paper · Lunanele Holdings

Executive Summary

South Africa loses an estimated 30% of agricultural produce annually to inadequate post-harvest handling and cold chain infrastructure. This is not a peripheral inefficiency — it is a structural failure of national significance, with direct consequences for food security, agricultural sector profitability, and export competitiveness.

The Investment Thesis

Cold chain infrastructure occupies a unique position in the investment landscape: it is simultaneously a productive operational asset, a critical national infrastructure component, and a direct food security intervention. This triple characteristic makes it attractive across institutional investor categories — from commercial capital seeking yield to development finance mandates seeking impact alignment.

Cold storage facilities generate stable, long-duration revenue through storage fees, logistics throughput, and value-added processing charges. Their asset values are supported by replacement cost floors and structural demand that does not diminish in economic downturns — food is not discretionary.

Lunanele Agribusiness's Role

Lunanele Agribusiness is positioned to develop, own, and operate cold chain infrastructure as the anchor of its agricultural mandate. The subsidiary's model integrates cold chain with upstream commercial farming and downstream agro-processing — creating a vertically integrated value chain that captures margin at multiple stages and reduces single-point-of-failure risk.

Conclusion

Cold chain investment is not niche infrastructure. It is foundational infrastructure that South Africa's agricultural economy cannot function without. Investors who recognise this early participate in assets with structural demand, long operational lifespans, and the institutional backing of a group whose mandate explicitly aligns with national food security priorities.

The Holding Company as a Development Finance Vehicle
Capital Markets · Position Paper · Lunanele Holdings

Executive Summary

Development finance institutions and sovereign capital bodies in South Africa have historically deployed capital through single-sector funds, project-specific vehicles, or direct balance sheet lending. Each of these structures carries inherent limitations in the context of long-horizon developmental investment in an environment as complex and multi-dimensional as South Africa's.

The Structural Argument

The diversified holding company offers a superior architecture for developmental capital deployment for three structural reasons. First, it distributes risk across genuinely distinct sectors, reducing correlation between capital at risk. Second, it enables capital reallocation between subsidiaries as performance differentials emerge — a feature unavailable in sector-specific vehicles. Third, it provides a single, governed counterpart for institutional investors rather than requiring them to manage relationships across multiple project entities.

These characteristics make the holding company model particularly well-suited to the mandates of DFIs, pension funds, and sovereign wealth vehicles — all of which require consolidated governance, long-duration exposure, and the flexibility to participate in economy-wide development rather than being confined to a single sector's performance.

The Lunanele Group as a Model

The Lunanele Group was constituted as a holding company precisely because its founders recognised that the challenges South Africa faces — infrastructure, food security, digital transformation, capital market development — cannot be addressed in isolation. They are interconnected, and the capital structure that addresses them must be interconnected in the same way.

Conclusion

The question for institutional investors is not whether the holding company model is appropriate for developmental investment — it demonstrably is. The question is whether the specific holding company in question has the governance, the sector portfolio, and the long-horizon commitment to serve as a credible developmental capital vehicle. The Lunanele Group was built to answer that question affirmatively.

Due Diligence — Q&A

Questions Institutional Parties Actually Ask

This section addresses the substantive questions posed by development finance institutions, sovereign capital bodies, and institutional investors during formal engagement processes. Each answer goes beyond what is stated elsewhere on this site.

How does the group's mandate align with South Africa's National Development Plan?
The group's five sectors are not incidental to the NDP — they reflect a deliberate alignment with the plan's infrastructure build programme, agricultural transformation targets, digital economy priorities, and employment creation imperatives. Lunanele Infrastructure addresses the NDP's infrastructure deficit. Lunanele Agribusiness addresses food security and smallholder integration. Lunanele Technology addresses the digital transition. This alignment is structural, not rhetorical.
Can the group enter into formal bilateral arrangements with public sector entities and state enterprises?
Yes. The group is constituted and structured to support formal bilateral arrangements with public institutions, state enterprises, and organs of state. These arrangements may take the form of service delivery partnerships, joint development agreements, co-investment vehicles, or preferential supplier relationships. All such arrangements are subject to formal engagement, appropriate disclosure, and bilateral agreement on terms.
What is the group's B-BBEE positioning?
The group is in the process of formalising its B-BBEE compliance framework in alignment with the applicable sector codes. Full verification and certification will be completed as part of the group's institutional readiness programme. Partners requiring specific B-BBEE documentation as part of engagement are encouraged to raise this requirement directly through the formal enquiry process.
Can the group receive Development Finance Institution funding or co-investment?
The group's structure is compatible with DFI co-investment frameworks, including blended finance arrangements, subordinated debt instruments, and equity participation through development-aligned vehicles. Engagements with the DBSA, IDC, NEF, SEFA, and equivalent institutions are welcomed and can be initiated through the formal enquiry channel on this site.
How does the group contribute to employment and local economic development?
Employment creation is embedded in the operational model of three subsidiaries in particular — Agribusiness, Infrastructure, and Technology. Agribusiness and Infrastructure operations are inherently labour-intensive. The group's procurement philosophy prioritises local suppliers and contractors. As the group scales, formal LED reporting and community development commitments will be formalised within each subsidiary's operating charter.
What category of investor is this group structured to engage?
The group engages qualified institutional investors, family offices, development-oriented capital vehicles, and high-net-worth individuals whose investment horizons and risk tolerances are appropriate for a long-duration, diversified holding group in an emerging market context. The group does not solicit retail capital or short-term speculative investment. All capital participation arrangements are subject to formal bilateral assessment.
What return structures are available to institutional investors?
Return structures are negotiated on a case-by-case basis based on the nature of the participation, the duration of commitment, and the specific subsidiary or group-level instrument through which the investment is structured. Possible structures include equity participation, profit-sharing arrangements, subordinated debt with defined returns, and hybrid instruments. No standard return is published because the group does not operate a uniform investment product.
How is capital protected within the group structure?
Capital protection mechanisms are defined at the instrument level during the structuring of each participation arrangement. The holding group's centralised capital governance provides consolidated oversight, preventing any single subsidiary from creating liability that uncontrollably propagates to the holding entity or other subsidiaries. Structural protection, collateral, and governance provisions are addressed as part of formal engagement.
Is there an exit mechanism for institutional investors?
Exit provisions are defined within the terms of each specific participation arrangement. The group does not offer an open-market exit mechanism — there is no listed security and no public secondary market for group instruments. Exit arrangements, including buyback provisions, secondary transfers to approved counterparts, or structured wind-down provisions, are negotiated as part of the initial participation agreement.
When does the group anticipate a public listing or significant liquidity event?
There is no current commitment to a listing timeline. A JSE or equivalent listing would be considered at an appropriate stage of the group's maturity and only where it serves the group's long-term strategic interests rather than providing a short-term liquidity mechanism for early participants. Institutional investors who require a defined liquidity event within a specific timeframe should disclose this requirement at the outset of engagement.
How can the group's registration be independently verified?
Lunanele Holdings Pty Ltd and all five wholly-owned subsidiaries are registered with the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC) of the Republic of South Africa. Company registration documentation is available on request through the formal enquiry channel. Parties are encouraged to conduct their own CIPC verification, and the group will cooperate fully with any reasonable due diligence process.
Who are the principals and decision-makers within the group?
The group is founder-led. Institutional counterparts engage directly with the principals who hold decision-making authority — there are no intermediary structures or delegated agents presenting on behalf of undisclosed principals. Formal disclosure of directorship, beneficial ownership, and organisational structure is provided as part of any structured engagement process upon request.
What financial information is available to qualified parties?
The group is in active build phase and does not publish audited financial statements publicly at this stage. Qualified institutional parties who have entered a formal engagement process and executed appropriate confidentiality agreements may request access to available financial documentation, management accounts, and subsidiary-level financial information as part of a structured due diligence process.
How does the group handle conflicts of interest and related-party transactions?
All inter-subsidiary transactions and related-party arrangements are governed by the group's internal capital governance framework, administered at the holding company level. Transactions between subsidiaries are conducted on arm's-length terms and subject to holding-level review. This framework will be formalised within auditable governance documentation as part of the group's institutional readiness programme.
Is information submitted during enquiry treated as confidential?
All information submitted through formal enquiry channels is treated with full confidentiality. It is used exclusively to respond to and progress the specific engagement and is not disclosed to third parties, circulated beyond those directly responsible for the engagement, or retained beyond the period necessary for the purpose for which it was submitted. Formal NDA arrangements are available where counterparts require mutual confidentiality obligations prior to substantive discussion.

Privacy Policy

1. Who We Are

This Privacy Policy applies to Lunanele Holdings Pty Ltd and its five wholly-owned subsidiaries, collectively referred to as "the Group." The Group is registered in the Republic of South Africa and is subject to the Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA), Act 4 of 2013.

2. Information We Collect

The Group collects personal information submitted through enquiry forms, NDA execution, appointment scheduling, and supplier registration on this website. This includes names, email addresses, telephone numbers, organisational affiliations, and the content of your communications with us.

3. How We Use Your Information

Information submitted is used exclusively to respond to and progress the specific engagement for which it was submitted. We do not sell, share, or distribute personal information to third parties except where required by law or as necessary to progress a formal engagement with your explicit knowledge.

4. Data Retention

Personal information is retained for as long as necessary to fulfil the purpose for which it was collected, or as required by applicable law. Enquiry information is retained for a minimum of 12 months to maintain engagement records. NDA execution records are retained for the duration of the agreement plus a period of five years.

5. Your Rights Under POPIA

You have the right to request access to personal information we hold about you, to request correction of inaccurate information, and to request deletion of your information where no legal obligation requires its retention. Requests may be submitted through the formal enquiry channel on this website.

6. Security

The Group implements reasonable technical and organisational measures to protect personal information against unauthorised access, disclosure, alteration, or destruction. All enquiry data is handled with institutional-grade confidentiality standards.

7. Cookies

This website uses minimal functional cookies necessary for basic site operation. No tracking cookies, advertising cookies, or third-party analytics cookies are deployed. By continuing to use this site, you accept the use of functional cookies as described.

8. Contact

For all privacy-related enquiries, please contact the Group's Information Officer through the formal enquiry channel on this website, selecting "General Enquiry" as the category and referencing POPIA in your submission.
